Condoms: The Proven Classic

Consistent and correct use of condoms is one of the most effective ways to prevent HIV and other sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

  • How they work: Create a barrier that stops the exchange of bodily fluids during sex.
  • Effectiveness: Over 99% effective when used perfectly.
  • Key Tip: Use a new condom every time for vaginal, anal, and oral sex.
üíä

PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis): The Proactive Pill

PrEP is a daily pill for HIV-negative people at high risk of getting HIV. It is a powerful tool to take control of your sexual health.

  • How it works: When taken as prescribed, PrEP builds up in your body to block HIV from establishing an infection.
  • Effectiveness: Over 99% effective at preventing HIV from sex.
  • Key Tip: You must be prescribed PrEP by a healthcare provider.
üöë

PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis): The Emergency Backup

PEP is for emergency situations. It is a short course of antiretroviral drugs taken after a possible exposure to HIV.

  • How it works: A 28-day course of medication that must be started within 72 hours of exposure.
  • Effectiveness: Highly effective if started promptly.
  • Key Tip: TIME IS CRITICAL. Do not wait. Contact a clinic or emergency room immediately.

2. Protecting the Next Generation: Mother-to-Child Prevention

An HIV-positive mother can significantly reduce the risk of passing HIV to her baby during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding. With proper intervention, the risk can be less than 1%.

Antiretroviral Therapy
Medicine for Baby
Safe Feeding

Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

The mother takes HIV medicine throughout pregnancy and childbirth. This keeps her viral load (amount of HIV in the blood) low, protecting the baby.

Viral loadThe amount of HIV in a blood sample suppression is key to preventing transmission to the baby.

HIV Medicine for the Baby

The newborn receives HIV medicine for 4-6 weeks after birth to provide additional protection against any potential exposure during delivery.
