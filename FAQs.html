Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between HIV and AIDS?

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) attacks the immune system. AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) is the most advanced stage of HIV infection, characterized by a severely weakened immune system and the occurrence of opportunistic infections.

How is HIV transmitted?

HIV is transmitted via blood, semen, vaginal fluids, rectal fluids, and breast milk. Common transmission routes include unprotected sex, sharing needles, and from mother to child during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding. It cannot be spread through casual contact like hugging, shaking hands, or sharing utensils.

Can HIV be prevented?

Yes. Prevention methods include practicing safe sex with condoms, not sharing needles, taking PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) if at high risk, and ensuring blood products are screened. PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) can also be taken within 72 hours after potential exposure.

Is there a cure for HIV?

There is currently no cure for HIV, but antiretroviral therapy (ART) allows people with HIV to live long, healthy lives. ART reduces the viral load to undetectable levels, which also prevents transmission to others (U=U). Research continues toward finding a cure.

Why is regular HIV testing important?

Regular testing helps with early diagnosis, which leads to timely treatment and better health outcomes. It also reduces the risk of transmission to others. The CDC recommends that everyone between the ages of 13 and 64 get tested for HIV at least once as part of routine health care.

What does U=U mean?

U=U stands for "Undetectable = Untransmittable." It means that people with HIV who achieve and maintain an undetectable viral load by taking antiretroviral therapy as prescribed cannot sexually transmit HIV to others.

How soon after exposure can HIV be detected?

It depends on the test. Nucleic acid tests (NAT) can detect HIV 10-33 days after exposure. Antigen/antibody tests (lab-based) can detect HIV 18-45 days after exposure. Rapid tests and self-tests (antibody tests) can detect HIV 23-90 days after exposure.

Can I get HIV from oral sex?

The risk of getting HIV from oral sex is much lower than from vaginal or anal sex, but it is not zero. The risk can increase if there are cuts or sores in the mouth or on the genitals. Using condoms or dental dams can reduce this risk.
